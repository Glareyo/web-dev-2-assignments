An Interview with a Sales Associate

How did you get into the industry?

In the beginning, he first began in 1982 working with Tom James Clothiers, which he landed from “a friend of a friend”.

As he continued his profession, he eventually continues his own, buying 3 bespoke tailor shops and developing from there into the Montopli Custom Clothiers today.

When entering Custom Clothier Sales, what did you find challenging at first?

“Finding new Clients”

For him, from the start of his career to now, a big challenge that continuously presents itself is the gathering of new clientele.

Beforehand, he would have to cold-call people, attempting to get clients via calling them in hopes of capturing an interest.

Today, he says it is even harder, as now rather than cold calling, the current method is now creating a presentable presence on social media.

A way to help overcome this continuous challenge is follow-ups. Calling old clientele to see if they are interesting inn continuing purchasing some additional suits.

When working with a client, what questions were the most difficult to answer?

To him, he describes it as trying to be “kind of like a psychiatrist.”

To clarify, getting to know the client via conversations and consultations:

- What does the client like?

- What is the client looking for?

- Where is the client coming from?

- And What does the client want?

What usually gets you motivated to sell a suit to a client?

For him, one reason is getting to make the client look and feel good in a suit, describing it as being interested in the client.

To further explain, he favors talking to the clients to get to know them, but using this, to “...pull them out of that comfortable zone...”.

Alongside this, he focuses on making sure that the client gets what they want, and that they look good in what they buy.

In a way, to hopefully have the client get compliments from other viewers.

What top tools should every suit salesperson have in Clothing Selling?

The usage of social media.

As described from the most difficult challenge of being a Suits Salesperson, he says that social media is one of the tools a Salesperson should utilize.

As he reasons, it is mainly that “...people rely on feedback...”.

To clarify, being able to present Montopoli and their suits in a presentable manner on an online presence.

It is important to present excellent quality and presence, as he described the Custom and Bespoke clothing business as “...the easiest business to get into, the hardest business to keep.”

What are some pitfalls that a Custom Clothier salesman should watch out for?

Sticking to only one style.

In business and in general, different people want different things. This could range from more traditional suits and styles to more fancier and trendy suits and styles.

As he describes, a pitfall that a Bespoke and Custom salesperson should avoid is sticking to only one style.

The best way to overcome it is by being flexible and able to work with a wide variety of styles. This would help cover a wider array of clients, such as what works for them and their lifestyle, and henceforth to build a bigger clientele.

In the Chicagoland area, there are several other Men’s Suit stores, such as Zeglio, Jacob Young Custom Clothiers, Daniel George Custom Clothiers, and Balani.

Is it important to know what other Custom Clothiers / Competitions offer?

It is important to know what each competitor offers.

But not only in terms of fabric or what you offer, but in terms of

“...What do you stand for?”.

“...Why would someone come to you?”

“...How you handle the problems...”

In different clothier websites, they each take different numbers of measurements

In your experience/opinion, are there a group or handful of specific measurements that a Suits Salesman should know and master when measuring someone for a suit?

To ensure a proper fit, 25+ measurements sound like the basic minimum in general, as he explains.

The slope of the shoulders is important, as the determine if a client as peaked or square shoulders, and how they stand, which is the “...backbone of the garment...”

But furthermore, he explains that to ensure a proper fit, “...A proper fit not only takes the measurements, but how you interpret those measurements...”.

Because of this, it is important to know how to interpret and communicate these measurements to the tailors, so that the client gets the proper fit, as bespoke garments take a bit longer to create vs Bin Garments

(Bin Garments : Garments that are taken from general stock and sewn together.)

As stated on the website, it mentions that it is possible to schedule an appointment for in-home appointments or outside locations.

What are some difficulties that the salesperson can come across in out-of-store consultations?

It all falls into how organized the Custom and Bespoke Salesperson is.

In both business and presentation.

To specify, what fabrics to bring, what to show, and “...how to compartmentalize things...”, all which is based on the client themselves.

In general, to create a system of how you would present the clothing you have.

And to be sure to write things down, as to now forget.
